Mesmo ambiente, cenários diferentes

What makes a city have better maternal and neonatal health indicators compared to neighboring cities?

Let us consider the examples of Santo André, São Bernardo and São Caetano, three neighboring cities in the Greater São Paulo region.

The three municipalities have similar geographic and urban conditions.

Air pollution is one of these conditions: The three had high annual mean concentration of fine particulate matter (PM2.5) in 2019, around 51.1 µg/m³.

This level of pollution is more than ten times higher than what is considered “no harmful pollution” according to the World Health Organization (WHO) air quality guidelines.

Source: World Health Organization. Air Quality Guidelines - Update 2021. Copenhagen, Denmark: WHO Regional Office for Europe (2021).

Prolonged exposure to unhealthy air is associated with worse conditions in people with vascular and respiratory diseases. Would it have a greater effect on pregnant women with an underlying condition?

On the other hand, if we look at birth weight, we see that the three cities show quite different variations.

The same goes for babies born small vis-à-vis the gestational age. Is it possible that environmental pollution has some effect on this variability?

We are still trying to understand how and in what ways these indicators relate -- and these discoveries are critical to the health of future and current generations.
